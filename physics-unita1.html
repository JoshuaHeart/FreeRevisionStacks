Kinematics

Foundational motion analysis for the IB Physics HL Syllabus.

1. Basic Definitions

These terms will be used in every physics unit, memorise them and understand them properly.

  • Distance (\(d\)): a scalar quantity, is the total length of the path travelled by an object between two points. Measured in meters (m). Since distance is a scalar quantity, it can only be positive.
  • Displacement (\(s\) or \(\Delta x\)): a vector quantity, is the change in position. It is a straight line from the initial position to the final position, (with direction). Measured in meters (m). Since displacement is a vector quantity, it can be negative or positive.

The 400m Runner Example

The distance a 400m runner runs is 400m, but since they finish at the same place they started, the displacement is 0m.

  • Speed (\(v\)): A scalar quantity, change in distance per unit time. Measured in meters per second (m/s).

    \(v = \text{total distance} / \text{total time}\)

  • Velocity (\(v\)): a vector quantity, the rate of change of displacement with respect to time, or simply the change in displacement per unit time. Essentially how quickly the displacement of an object changes. Measured in meters per second (m/s).

    \(v = ds/dt\)

  • Acceleration (\(a\)): A vector quantity, the rate at which velocity changes per unit time. Measured in meters per second squared (m/s²). An object accelerates any time its velocity changes (either direction or magnitude).

    \(a = dv/dt = d^2s/dt^2\)

3. Kinematic (SUVAT) Equations

Note: Derivations are outside of the scope of IB, and they are just here to aid understanding.

\(v = u + at\) Detailed Derivation

This is derived directly from the definition of acceleration as the rate of change of velocity:

\[a = \frac{\Delta v}{t} = \frac{v - u}{t}\]

By multiplying both sides by \(t\), we get \(at = v - u\). Adding \(u\) to both sides yields the final equation: \(\mathbf{v = u + at}\).

\(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\) Detailed Derivation

This stems from finding the area under a Velocity-Time graph. The area of the trapezoid is:

\[s = \text{Area} = \frac{u+v}{2} \cdot t\]

We substitute our first equation (\(v = u + at\)) for \(v\):

\[s = \frac{u + (u + at)}{2} \cdot t = \frac{2u + at}{2} \cdot t\]

\[s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\]

\(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\) Detailed Derivation

We derive this by eliminating the time variable (\(t\)). Rearrange the first equation to solve for \(t\):

\[t = \frac{v-u}{a}\]

Substitute this into the displacement equation \(s = \frac{u+v}{2}t\):

\[s = \left(\frac{v+u}{2}\right)\left(\frac{v-u}{a}\right) = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a}\]

\[2as = v^2 - u^2 \implies v^2 = u^2 + 2as\]

5. Terminal Velocity (Skydiver Analysis)

The Skydiver Graph

Graph Breakdown:

  • \(t=0\): Gradient is steepest (\(g = 9.81 \, m/s^2\)) because Drag is zero.
  • Middle Curve: The gradient decreases. As velocity increases, Air Resistance increases, reducing the net force (\(W-D\)) and thus reducing acceleration.
  • Plateau: The gradient becomes zero. Drag equals Weight (\(D=W\)). Net force is zero.

The Parachute Effect

When the parachute opens, Surface Area increases dramatically. Drag becomes much larger than Weight.

  • The skydiver decelerates rapidly (velocity drops).
  • As velocity drops, Drag decreases.
  • Eventually, Drag equals Weight again at a **lower terminal velocity**, allowing for a safe landing.
