1. Basic Definitions

These terms will be used in every physics unit, memorise them and understand them properly[cite: 1].

  • Distance (\(d\)): a scalar quantity, is the total length of the path travelled by an object between two points[cite: 1]. Measured in meters (m)[cite: 2]. Since distance is a scalar quantity, it can only be positive[cite: 5].
  • Displacement (\(s\) or \(\Delta x\)): a vector quantity, is the change in position[cite: 1]. It is a straight line from the initial position to the final position, (with direction)[cite: 3]. Measured in meters (m)[cite: 3]. Since displacement is a vector quantity, it can be negative or positive[cite: 6].

The 400m Runner Example

The distance a 400m runner runs is 400m, but since they finish at the same place they started, the displacement is 0m.

  • Speed (\(v\)): A scalar quantity, change in distance per unit time[cite: 8]. Measured in meters per second (m/s)[cite: 9].

    \(v = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}\)

  • Velocity (\(v\)): a vector quantity, the rate of change of displacement with respect to time, or simply the change in displacement per unit time[cite: 9]. Essentially how quickly the displacement of an object changes[cite: 10]. Measured in meters per second (m/s)[cite: 10].

    \(v = \frac{ds}{dt}\)

  • Acceleration (\(a\)): A vector quantity, the rate at which velocity changes per unit time[cite: 11]. Measured in meters per second squared (m/s²)[cite: 11]. An object accelerates any time its velocity changes (either direction or magnitude)[cite: 11].

    \(a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2}\)

3. Kinematic (SUVAT) Equations

Note: Derivations are outside of the scope of IB, and they are just here to aid understanding[cite: 19].

\(v = u + at\) View Derivation

Starting with \(v_f = v_i + \Delta v\)

Since \(a = \frac{\Delta v}{t}\), then \(\Delta v = at\)

Substitute back: \(\mathbf{v = u + at}\)

\(s = \frac{u+v}{2}t\) View Derivation

Average velocity is \(\bar{v} = \frac{u+v}{2}\)

Distance is displacement over time: \(s = \bar{v}t\)

Substitute \(\bar{v}\): \(\mathbf{s = \frac{u+v}{2}t}\)

\(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\) View Derivation

Start with \(v = u + at\)

Integrate velocity with respect to time: \(\int v dt = \int (u + at) dt\)

Evaluating the integral gives: \(\mathbf{s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2}\)

\(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\) View Derivation

From \(a = \frac{v-u}{t}\), we get \(t = \frac{v-u}{a}\)

Substitute this into \(s = \bar{v}t\): \(s = \left(\frac{v+u}{2}\right)\left(\frac{v-u}{a}\right)\)

\(s = \frac{(v+u)(v-u)}{2a} = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2a}\)

Rearranging: \(2as = v^2 - u^2 \implies \mathbf{v^2 = u^2 + 2as}\)

Methodology [cite: 20, 22, 23]

  1. List which SUVAT values you know (with units).
  2. Decompose any diagonal vectors into horizontal (\(x\)) and vertical (\(y\)) planes using trigonometry.
  3. Select an equation that contains all known values plus the one unknown you seek.
  4. Rearrange and solve.

4. Projectiles & Vectors

The velocity vector in the \(y\) direction acts independently of the velocity vector in the \(x\) direction and vice versa. We decompose vectors using sine and cosine functions.

Example: Calculating Launch Components

A ball is launched at \(20 \, m/s\) at an angle of \(30°\) to the horizontal.

  • Horizontal Velocity (\(u_x\)): \(20 \cos(30°) = 17.3 \, m/s\)
  • Vertical Velocity (\(u_y\)): \(20 \sin(30°) = 10.0 \, m/s\)

Independence: If there is no air resistance, \(u_x\) remains \(17.3 \, m/s\) for the entire flight, while \(u_y\) changes due to gravity acting downwards at \(9.81 \, m/s^2\).

5. Air Resistance & Terminal Velocity

Air resistance is a force always opposing motion, acting in the opposite direction to an object’s velocity. Greater magnitude of velocity means a greater air resistance.

Skydiver Velocity Graph

Terminal Velocity: Where air resistance is equal to the force of gravity; net force is zero, and thus the object does not accelerate[cite: 25, 26].

Parachute Deployment: When the parachute opens, drag increases instantly. The object decelerates rapidly until reaching a new, lower terminal velocity.
