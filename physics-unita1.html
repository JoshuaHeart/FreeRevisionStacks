Kinematics

Comprehensive notes on motion analysis based on the IB Physics HL Syllabus.

1. Basic Definitions

Memorize and understand these terms properly; they are used in every physics unit.

Distance (\(d\)): A scalar quantity representing the total length of the path travelled by an object. Measured in meters (m). Since it is scalar, it can only be positive.

Displacement (\(s\) or \(\Delta x\)): A vector quantity representing the change in position. It is a straight line from the initial position to the final position with direction. Measured in meters (m). As a vector, it can be positive or negative.

The 400m Runner Example

The distance a 400m runner runs is 400m, but since they finish at the same place they started, their displacement is 0m.

Speed (\(v\)): Scalar quantity; the total distance covered per unit time.
\(v = \frac{\text{total distance}}{\text{total time}}\)

Velocity (\(v\)): Vector quantity; the rate of change of displacement with respect to time.
\(v_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} \quad \text{or} \quad v = \frac{ds}{dt}\)

Acceleration (\(a\)): Vector quantity; the rate at which velocity changes per unit time. An object accelerates any time its velocity changes in either direction or magnitude.
\(a_{\text{avg}} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} \quad \text{or} \quad a = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2s}{dt^2}\)

2. Motion Graphs

Displacement vs. Time

The gradient of the graph at any point \(t_0\) is the velocity at that point.

Velocity vs. Time

Gradient = Acceleration. Area under the graph = Displacement.

Acceleration vs. Time

The area under the graph between \(t_1\) and \(t_2\) represents the change in velocity.

3. Kinematic (SUVAT) Equations

s: disp. (m)
u: init. v (m/s)
v: final v (m/s)
a: accel. (m/s²)
t: time (s)

Note: Derivations are outside the scope of IB but included here to aid understanding.

\(v = u + at\)
From definition \(a = \frac{v-u}{t}\). Rearrange to get \(at = v-u\).
\(s = \frac{(u+v)t}{2}\)
Average velocity \(\frac{u+v}{2}\) multiplied by time.
\(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
Substitute \(v=u+at\) into \(s = \frac{u+v}{2}t\).
\(s = vt - \frac{1}{2}at^2\)
Substitute \(u=v-at\) into \(s = \frac{u+v}{2}t\).
\(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\)
Substitute \(t = \frac{v-u}{a}\) into \(s = \frac{u+v}{2}t\).

Problem Solving Tips

  • Always list your known SUVAT values first.
  • Choose the equation that contains all your known values plus the one unknown you need.
  • Because values (except time) are vectors, you can decompose them into x and y planes using trig.

4. Projectiles & Vectors

Velocity in the y-direction acts independently of the x-direction. Decompose vectors using sine and cosine.

\(v_x = v \cos\theta \quad | \quad v_y = v \sin\theta\)

In projectile motion (no air resistance), horizontal velocity (\(v_x\)) is constant while gravity acts downwards on \(v_y\).

5. Air Resistance & Terminal Velocity

Air resistance is a force that always opposes motion. Greater velocity magnitude results in greater air resistance.

Terminal Velocity

This is where air resistance equals the force of gravity. The net force becomes zero, acceleration stops, and the object reaches its maximum constant speed.
